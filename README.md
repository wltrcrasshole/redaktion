# Redaktion

### An AI editorial assistant for editors

*Building AI course project*

---

## Summary

Redaktion is an AI-powered editorial assistant that helps editors generate, evaluate, store, and track story ideas – so nothing gets lost and every issue feels intentional. It combines a persistent idea bank with AI-powered planning to support smarter, faster editorial decision-making across any publication. The working prototype is a Python command-line tool powered by the Claude API.

---

## Background

Editorial idea management is a problem hiding in plain sight. Most magazines, newspapers, and media outlets run on the creativity and instincts of their editors – but the systems used to capture, develop, and act on ideas are often shockingly informal: a shared document nobody maintains, a notes app, or simply someone's memory.

The consequences are real and recurring. When ideas aren't captured and tracked properly, assignments go out late, contributors are briefed poorly or not at all, and story opportunities tied to events or timing are missed entirely. Meeting time gets consumed by starting from scratch rather than building on what already exists. Ideas get accidentally duplicated – or claimed by someone else. And perhaps most damaging of all: the publication never grows. Without a system for nurturing longer-term ideas, magazines default to the familiar, repeating the same kinds of stories issue after issue.

This problem is common across the entire media industry, from large mastheads to small independent publications. It is felt most acutely in leaner editorial teams where no one person has the bandwidth to be both creative and organized.

My personal motivation comes from working as an editor at a small, independent print magazine. The work is meaningful and the team is passionate – but the workflows are outdated, and good ideas too often disappear before they ever become stories. I want to change that, starting with a tool I would actually use myself.

## Data and AI Techniques

Redaktion depends on one primary data source in the current prototype, with a second planned for future development.

**The idea bank** – a structured JSON file maintained by the editor, containing story ideas in various states: unused, pitched, held, published, or rejected. Each entry includes a title, description, section, status, format (print, online, or both), issue history, contributor attribution, and published URL where applicable. This is the editorial memory that Redaktion draws on when planning an issue. Ideas accumulate over time from three sources: the editor's own thinking, writer pitches logged through the tool, and new suggestions generated by Claude during planning sessions.

**Live content feeds** – RSS feeds or news APIs that surface current events and trending topics relevant to the publication's focus are planned for a future version. In the case of a queer Berlin publication, these might include local news sources, community organisation feeds, and social media signals. This would allow Redaktion to flag timely stories that aren't date-driven but are happening right now.

The core AI technique is **natural language processing**, powered by a large language model (Claude API by Anthropic). The model is given the magazine's identity, editorial values, and coverage window, then reads the idea bank to produce a suggested issue plan. It prioritises existing and pitched ideas before proposing new ones, and adapts its suggestions to the publication's specific community and cultural context. Redaktion's magazine identity is fully configurable via a `config.json` file, making the tool usable by any publication without touching the code.

A **feedback loop** allows the system to build editorial memory over time. After each issue closes, the editor reports what actually ran, what was held, and what was dropped. The idea bank updates accordingly, so future planning sessions are informed by real decisions rather than starting from scratch.

Supporting techniques include basic **recommendation logic** (matching ideas to coverage windows based on metadata and semantic relevance) and **status tracking** (maintaining a full lifecycle for each idea from first pitch to publication).

## How It Is Used

Redaktion is used by editors across two moments in the editorial cycle, not just one.

The first is the **planning phase** – typically the period between closing one issue and beginning active production on the next. The editor opens the tool, inputs the upcoming coverage window and available slot count, and receives a suggested issue plan drawn from the idea bank. It is a thinking partner, not a decision-maker. Every suggestion it makes is subject to human judgment, and the editor retains full creative and editorial control. Writers can also submit pitches into Redaktion at any point via the pitch logging flow, which stores them in the idea bank for evaluation during the next planning session.

The second is the **closing phase** – after the issue is locked and decisions are final. The editor reports back to Redaktion what actually ran: which ideas were published (in print, online, or both), which are being held for a future issue, and which have been rejected. Redaktion uses this input to update the idea bank and ensure future planning sessions reflect what has already been covered. Variations or fresh angles on previously covered topics remain welcome — the goal is informed planning, not rigid restriction.

The effects of better editorial planning ripple outward to everyone involved in making the magazine. Writers benefit from clearer, earlier briefs and more lead time. Photographers and designers can be brought into the process sooner when the shape of an issue is decided earlier. The editor-in-chief gains a more considered and defensible issue plan – though a well-functioning Redaktion might occasionally present the welcome problem of too many strong options for a limited number of pages.

The most sensitive stakeholder is the reader. In a journalism context, any involvement of AI in the editorial process raises legitimate questions about integrity, bias, and authenticity. It is important to be clear: Redaktion does not write stories, select facts, or make editorial judgments about what is true or important. It helps humans organise and develop ideas faster and more systematically. The journalism itself – the reporting, the writing, the editing – remains entirely human. Editors and writers using Redaktion should be prepared to explain this distinction openly if asked.

## Challenges

Redaktion addresses a real and recurring problem in editorial workflows, but it has meaningful limitations that are worth stating honestly.

**The risk of uncritical adoption** is perhaps the most immediate. An AI suggesting story ideas will inevitably produce some generic or safe options alongside genuinely useful ones. If editors and writers accept suggestions without critically evaluating them, the result could be a publication that feels increasingly formulaic – the opposite of what Redaktion is designed to prevent. The tool is only as good as the editorial judgment applied to its output.

**Adoption and the feedback loop problem** are closely related. If Redaktion is too technical or cumbersome to use comfortably, editors may simply stop engaging with it. This creates a damaging spiral: no engagement means no feedback, no feedback means the idea bank never learns, and a system that never learns keeps producing the same suggestions until it loses credibility entirely. Ease of use is therefore not just a design consideration but a functional one.

**Data quality and source credibility** will matter enormously when live feed integration is added. If Redaktion is pointed at unreliable, sensationalist, or politically suspect sources, it will surface misleading or harmful story signals. The system is only as trustworthy as the sources it monitors, and curating those sources requires ongoing human oversight.

**Cultural and community nuance** is a genuine limitation of any large language model. A publication serving a specific community – in this case the queer community in Berlin – relies on a deep understanding of context, terminology, lived experience, and sensitivity that an AI may handle clumsily or incorrectly. Editors must remain the cultural authority, not the model.

**Bias in the idea bank** is a subtler risk. If the ideas fed into Redaktion reflect past editorial blind spots or underrepresentation, the system will tend to reproduce and reinforce those patterns rather than challenge them. Diverse and critically curated input is essential.

**The interface gap** remains a real limitation. The command-line interface assumes a degree of technical comfort that many editorial professionals don't have and shouldn't need. The prototype proves the concept and is functional for technically comfortable users; a clean web-based or mobile interface would make it genuinely accessible to the full range of people who could benefit from it.

Finally, **privacy and data handling** deserve attention. Any future version that scans social media or news feeds would raise questions about what data is being retrieved, stored, and processed. These questions would need to be addressed carefully and transparently before any broader deployment.

## What Next

Redaktion begins as a personal tool for a single editor, but its natural trajectory is outward – toward teams, publications, and eventually communities.

**The immediate next step** is addressing the interface gap identified in Challenges. A proper web-based or mobile interface would make Redaktion accessible to editors without any technical background, which is essential for real-world adoption. The command-line prototype proves the concept; a clean, intuitive UI would make it usable by anyone.

**Live trend integration** is the other near-term priority – connecting Redaktion to RSS feeds and news APIs so that planning sessions can incorporate what is happening right now, not just what is already in the idea bank.

**At the team level**, Redaktion's most significant potential lies in collaboration. A team-wide version would allow multiple editors and section heads to work within a shared system, each seeing what others are planning. If two sections are developing similar stories, Redaktion flags the overlap. If there is obvious potential for cooperation – a film review that connects to a community news story, for example – it suggests the synergy. The feedback loop becomes collective too: when one section publishes a story, every other section's planning is informed by it automatically.

**Calendar and production integration** would make Redaktion even more contextually aware – syncing with the magazine's actual deadlines, print dates, and lead times so that suggestions are always grounded in the real rhythm of production.

**Multilingual capability** is already present in the prototype – Claude responds in the language appropriate to each section – and would be developed further to support editorial teams working natively across multiple languages, making Redaktion genuinely useful for international and community publications worldwide.

**Over time**, Redaktion could develop analytical capabilities — showing editors patterns in their own decision-making, flagging underrepresented voices or themes, and helping publications become more intentional about the stories they tell and the communities they serve.

**The longest horizon** is cross-publication connection: a network where editors at different outlets can optionally signal what they are working on, creating opportunities for collaboration, coordination, and the kind of editorial synergy that is currently left entirely to chance and personal relationships.

In terms of who this is for: Redaktion is designed to be as useful to a small queer community magazine in Berlin as to a major national publication. If anything, the ambition is to prioritise accessibility for smaller, independent, and community-rooted outlets doing important work with limited resources. Larger publications are of course welcome – but they can expect to pay accordingly. 😉

## Acknowledgments

Redaktion was conceived from personal experience working as an editor at a small, independent print magazine. The idea emerged not from a single moment of inspiration but from the accumulated frustration of watching good ideas get lost, good stories go unpursued, and editorial energy get consumed by disorganisation at the expense of everything else a small publication needs to do – community building, visibility, growth, and sustainability. This project is dedicated to the editors, writers, photographers, and designers at small and independent media outlets who are doing meaningful work under real constraints.

The prototype is built using Python 3 and powered by the [Claude API](https://www.anthropic.com/api) by Anthropic. It uses the following libraries:

- [`anthropic`](https://pypi.org/project/anthropic/) — the official Python SDK for the Claude API, handling all AI calls
- [`json`](https://docs.python.org/3/library/json.html) — Python standard library module for reading and writing the idea bank and config files

No other third-party dependencies are required.

The [Building AI course](https://buildingai.elementsofai.com/) by the University of Helsinki and Reaktor provided the framework and encouragement to shape this idea into something concrete.
